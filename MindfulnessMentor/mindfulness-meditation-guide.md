## Getting Settled

First, find a quiet place where you won't be interrupted for the next 10 to 15 minutes. Silence your phone or set it to "Do Not Disturb." This is your time.

- **Choose your posture.** Sit in a way that feels both alert and relaxed. You can sit cross-legged on a cushion, upright in a chair with your feet flat on the floor, or even lie down if that feels better for you. The key is that your spine is gently straight --- not rigid, just naturally upright --- so your breath can flow freely.
- **Rest your hands.** Let them settle on your thighs, your knees, or in your lap. There's no "right" position --- just whatever feels natural.
- **Soften your gaze or close your eyes.** If closing your eyes feels uncomfortable, simply lower your gaze toward a point on the floor a few feet in front of you.

Take a moment to notice the feeling of your body making contact with the surface beneath you. Feel the weight of gravity holding you. You are supported. You don't need to be anywhere else right now.

## The Practice: Breath Awareness Meditation

### Step 1 --- Arrive with Three Deep Breaths

Begin by taking **three slow, deep breaths** to signal to your nervous system that it's time to slow down:

1. **Inhale** slowly through your nose for a count of four, letting your belly expand.
2. **Hold** gently at the top for a count of two.
3. **Exhale** slowly through your mouth for a count of six, letting your shoulders drop and your jaw relax.

Repeat this two more times. With each exhale, imagine releasing a little of the tension you've been carrying --- like setting down a heavy bag.

### Step 2 --- Return to Natural Breathing

Now let your breath return to its own natural rhythm. You don't need to control it or make it deeper or slower. Simply **observe** it, just as it is.

Bring your attention to the place where you feel the breath most clearly. This might be:

- The cool air entering your nostrils and the warm air flowing out
- The gentle rise and fall of your chest
- The expansion and contraction of your belly

Pick one of these anchor points and rest your attention there. You're not trying to think about the breath --- just feel it.

### Step 3 --- Notice When Your Mind Wanders

Within a few moments, your mind will probably wander. You might start thinking about your to-do list, something that happened earlier today, or what you're going to have for dinner. **This is completely normal.** It doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. In fact, noticing that your mind has wandered is itself a moment of mindfulness.

When you notice a thought has pulled you away, try this:

1. **Acknowledge** the thought gently. You might silently say to yourself, "Thinking," or "There's a thought."
2. **Let it go** without judging yourself or the thought. Imagine the thought is a cloud drifting across the sky, or a leaf floating by on a stream.
3. **Return** your attention to the feeling of your breath at your anchor point.

This cycle of noticing, releasing, and returning is the heart of the practice. Each time you do it, you're strengthening your ability to be present. Think of it like a bicep curl for your attention --- every return to the breath is one rep.

### Step 4 --- Expand Your Awareness (Body Scan)

After about five minutes of breath focus, gently widen your awareness to include your whole body. Without moving, scan from the top of your head down to your toes, noticing any sensations along the way:

- **Forehead and temples** --- Is there tightness here? Imagine it softening.
- **Jaw and mouth** --- Let your jaw hang slightly open. Unclench your teeth.
- **Shoulders and neck** --- These are common stress holders. Let your shoulders melt downward, away from your ears.
- **Hands** --- Notice if they're clenched. Open them gently.
- **Stomach and gut** --- Stress often lives here. Breathe into your belly and imagine warmth spreading through this area.
- **Legs and feet** --- Feel the contact of your feet with the floor. Feel grounded.

Wherever you find tension, don't fight it. Simply breathe into that area and give it permission to release. Some tension may soften immediately; some may not. Both are perfectly fine.

### Step 5 --- Rest in Open Awareness

For the final minute or two, let go of any specific focus. Don't concentrate on the breath or the body. Just **sit** and be aware. Notice sounds around you, the temperature of the air, the feeling of stillness. You might feel a quiet sense of spaciousness or peace. Or you might feel restless. Whatever arises, let it be.

This is the practice of simply being present, without an agenda.

### Step 6 --- Gently Close the Practice

When you feel ready:

1. Begin to deepen your breath again --- a few slow, nourishing inhales and exhales.
2. Gently wiggle your fingers and toes, reconnecting with your body.
3. If your eyes are closed, slowly open them, letting the light in gradually.
4. Take a moment to notice how you feel compared to when you started. You don't need to feel dramatically different --- even a subtle shift counts.

Before you stand up, set a small **intention** for the rest of your day. It might be something like: "I will take one conscious breath before responding to stressful situations," or "I will notice moments of beauty today." This bridges the calm of your practice into your daily life.

## Tips for Building a Sustainable Practice

- **Start small.** Five minutes a day is better than thirty minutes once a week. Consistency matters more than duration.
- **Anchor it to a habit.** Meditate right after something you already do every day --- after brushing your teeth, after your morning coffee, or right before bed.
- **Be compassionate with yourself.** There's no such thing as a "bad" meditation. If your mind wandered the entire time, you still practiced. You showed up, and that's what counts.
- **Track your practice.** A simple checkmark on a calendar each day you meditate can be surprisingly motivating.
- **Explore variety.** Once you're comfortable with breath awareness, we can explore other techniques like loving-kindness meditation, walking meditation, gratitude practice, or progressive muscle relaxation.

## A Quick "Stress Reset" for Busy Moments

If you ever need a fast reset during a hectic day and can't sit down for a full meditation, try this **90-second technique**:

1. **Pause** whatever you're doing.
2. **Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly.**
3. **Take five slow breaths**, feeling both hands rise and fall.
4. **Name three things you can sense right now** --- something you see, something you hear, and something you feel (like the texture of your clothing or the air on your skin).
5. **Continue** with your day.

This quick grounding exercise activates your parasympathetic nervous system and pulls you out of the stress response loop. It's remarkably effective and completely invisible to anyone around you.
