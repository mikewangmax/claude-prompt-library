# Lesson Plan: Introduction to Photosynthesis

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade (Ages 12-13)
**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Subject Area:** Life Science / Biology

---

## Lesson Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Define** photosynthesis and articulate why it is essential for life on Earth.
2. **Identify** the raw materials (carbon dioxide, water, light energy) and products (glucose, oxygen) of photosynthesis.
3. **Explain** the role of chloroplasts and chlorophyll in capturing light energy.
4. **Summarize** the overall chemical equation for photosynthesis in both word and symbolic form.
5. **Analyze** the relationship between photosynthesis and cellular respiration within an ecosystem.

**Aligned Standards:**
- NGSS MS-LS1-6: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for the role of photosynthesis in the cycling of matter and flow of energy into and out of organisms.
- NGSS MS-LS2-3: Develop a model to describe the cycling of matter and flow of energy among living and nonliving parts of an ecosystem.

---

## Materials and Resources

- Projector and computer with internet access
- Short video clip: "Photosynthesis Explained" (3-4 minutes, e.g., Crash Course Kids or Amoeba Sisters)
- Live potted plant and a flashlight
- Printed graphic organizer worksheets (one per student)
- Colored pencils or markers (one set per group)
- Large poster paper (one sheet per group)
- Index cards for exit tickets
- Whiteboard and dry-erase markers

---

## Lesson Outline

### I. Introduction (10 minutes)

#### A. Hook / Engagement Activity (5 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Interactive demonstration and questioning

- Hold up a live potted plant and ask students: *"What does this plant need to survive?"* Record student answers on the whiteboard (expect: water, soil, sunlight, air).
- Follow up with a thought-provoking question: *"We eat food to get energy. Plants don't eat anything -- so where does their energy come from?"*
- Allow 1-2 minutes of think-pair-share, where students discuss with a partner before sharing ideas with the class.

#### B. Video Introduction (3 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Multimedia instruction

- Play a short animated video (3-4 minutes) that provides a visual overview of photosynthesis.
- Instruct students to jot down at least two new facts they learn from the video on scrap paper.

#### C. Lesson Overview (2 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Direct instruction

- State the lesson objectives clearly on the board.
- Provide a brief roadmap: *"Today we will learn what photosynthesis is, what ingredients plants need, what they produce, and why this process matters for every living thing on Earth -- including us."*

---

### II. Main Activities (40 minutes)

#### A. Guided Instruction: The Science of Photosynthesis (12 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Interactive lecture with visual aids

1. **What is photosynthesis?**
   - Write the word on the board and break it into its Greek roots: *photo* (light) + *synthesis* (putting together).
   - Define: Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants and certain other organisms use sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose (food) and oxygen.

2. **Where does it happen?**
   - Show a diagram of a plant cell, zooming into the chloroplast.
   - Explain that chloroplasts contain **chlorophyll**, a green pigment that absorbs light energy. This is why most plants appear green.
   - Use the flashlight on the plant leaf to demonstrate how light interacts with the leaf surface.

3. **The photosynthesis equation:**
   - Write the word equation on the board:
     > Carbon Dioxide + Water ---(Light Energy)---> Glucose + Oxygen
   - Write the chemical equation:
     > 6CO₂ + 6H₂O ---(Light Energy)---> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
   - Walk through each component, using color-coding on the whiteboard (e.g., blue for water, gray for CO₂, yellow for light, orange for glucose, green for oxygen).

4. **Why does it matter?**
   - Briefly discuss that photosynthesis produces the oxygen we breathe and forms the base of nearly every food chain on Earth.
   - Pose the connection: *"Without photosynthesis, could animals -- including humans -- survive? Why or why not?"*

**Check for Understanding:** Ask students to give a thumbs-up/thumbs-down: "Photosynthesis produces carbon dioxide." (Answer: thumbs down -- it produces oxygen.) Use 2-3 quick true/false statements to gauge comprehension.

#### B. Small Group Activity: Photosynthesis Poster (18 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Collaborative, hands-on learning

1. **Setup (2 minutes):**
   - Divide the class into groups of 3-4 students.
   - Distribute poster paper, colored pencils/markers, and graphic organizer worksheets to each group.

2. **Task Instructions (1 minute):**
   - Each group must create a visual poster that illustrates the process of photosynthesis. The poster must include:
     - A labeled diagram showing inputs (CO₂, H₂O, light) and outputs (glucose, O₂)
     - The location within the plant (leaf, chloroplast, chlorophyll)
     - The word equation and/or chemical equation
     - At least one real-world example of why photosynthesis matters (e.g., food chains, oxygen production, carbon cycle)
   - Students should first plan their poster using the graphic organizer worksheet individually, then combine ideas as a group.

3. **Work Time (15 minutes):**
   - Circulate the room to monitor group progress, ask probing questions, and provide guidance.
   - Prompting questions to ask groups:
     - *"What would happen if you removed sunlight from the equation?"*
     - *"How does photosynthesis connect to what you already know about food chains?"*
     - *"Can you think of any organisms that photosynthesize but are not plants?"* (algae, some bacteria)

#### C. Gallery Walk and Class Discussion (10 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Peer learning and whole-class discussion

1. **Gallery Walk (5 minutes):**
   - Have groups tape their posters to the walls around the room.
   - Students rotate in groups to view each poster for about 1 minute each. Each student carries a sticky note to write one compliment or one question for each poster they visit.
   - Sticky notes are placed directly on the posters.

2. **Whole-Class Discussion (5 minutes):**
   - Bring the class back together. Invite 2-3 groups to briefly present their poster (1 minute each).
   - Address any recurring questions or misconceptions observed during the gallery walk.
   - Key misconceptions to address:
     - *"Plants only photosynthesize during the day"* -- True for the light-dependent reactions, but clarify that plants also respire at all times.
     - *"Soil is food for plants"* -- Clarify that soil provides minerals and water, but the plant's food (glucose) is made through photosynthesis.

---

### III. Conclusion (10 minutes)

#### A. Lesson Summary (3 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Direct instruction with student participation

- Collaboratively review key points by asking students to fill in the blanks:
  - *"Photosynthesis takes place in the ______ of plant cells."* (chloroplasts)
  - *"The green pigment that captures light is called ______."* (chlorophyll)
  - *"The two products of photosynthesis are ______ and ______."* (glucose and oxygen)
  - *"The three ingredients needed for photosynthesis are ______, ______, and ______."* (carbon dioxide, water, and light energy)

#### B. Exit Ticket Assessment (5 minutes)

**Teaching Method:** Individual written assessment

- Distribute index cards. Each student independently answers the following three questions:
  1. In your own words, define photosynthesis in one or two sentences.
  2. Write the word equation for photosynthesis.
  3. Explain one reason why photosynthesis is important for animals, including humans.

- Collect index cards as students leave.

#### C. Homework Preview (2 minutes)

- Assign a brief homework activity: *"Photosynthesis Detective"*
  - Students find three items in their home or surroundings that exist because of photosynthesis (e.g., a wooden table, a cotton shirt, an apple) and write 2-3 sentences explaining the connection to photosynthesis.
- Preview the next lesson: *"Next class, we will explore how photosynthesis and cellular respiration work together in a cycle, and we will conduct an experiment using aquatic plants to observe photosynthesis in action."*

## Assessment Methods

### Formative Assessments (During the Lesson)
| Method | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **Think-Pair-Share** (Introduction) | Prior knowledge and initial engagement with the topic |
| **Thumbs-Up/Thumbs-Down** (Guided Instruction) | Real-time comprehension of key facts |
| **Graphic Organizer Worksheet** (Group Activity) | Individual ability to organize and recall photosynthesis components |
| **Teacher Circulation & Questioning** (Group Activity) | Depth of understanding and ability to apply concepts |
| **Gallery Walk Sticky Notes** (Discussion) | Peer evaluation skills and identification of key concepts |

### Summative Assessments
| Method | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **Group Poster** | Collaborative understanding of the photosynthesis process, accuracy of labeling, and creativity in presentation |
| **Exit Ticket** | Individual mastery of lesson objectives -- definition, equation, and real-world significance |
| **Homework: Photosynthesis Detective** | Ability to transfer and apply classroom knowledge to real-world contexts |

### Grading Rubric for Exit Ticket (10 points total)
- **Definition of photosynthesis** (4 points): Accurate, complete, uses key vocabulary
- **Word equation** (3 points): All reactants and products correctly identified
- **Importance explanation** (3 points): Logical reasoning connecting photosynthesis to animal/human survival

---

## Differentiation Strategies

- **For struggling learners:** Provide a pre-filled graphic organizer with key terms and partial diagrams. Pair with a stronger peer during group work. Offer sentence starters for the exit ticket.
- **For advanced learners:** Challenge them to include the chemical formula on their poster and to research the difference between light-dependent and light-independent reactions. Ask them to write a paragraph comparing photosynthesis and cellular respiration.
- **For English Language Learners:** Provide a vocabulary list with definitions and visuals for key terms (photosynthesis, chloroplast, chlorophyll, glucose, carbon dioxide, oxygen). Allow use of bilingual dictionaries.

---

## Teacher Reflection Notes

After the lesson, consider:
- Did students achieve the learning objectives as evidenced by exit ticket responses?
- Which misconceptions were most common, and how can they be addressed in the next lesson?
- Were all students engaged during the group activity? How can groupings be adjusted for future collaborative work?
- Was the pacing appropriate, or did certain sections need more or less time?
